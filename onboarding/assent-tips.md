# Assent Tips for Caregivers

**Assent** means honoring a person's willingness—or refusal—to participate. In AAC, assent is not optional decoration; it is core safety.

## Recognize assent signals

**Yes signals (examples):**
- Reaching for the device
- Looking at a symbol you modeled
- Repeating a word or gesture
- Smiling or relaxing after a successful exchange

**No / pause signals (examples):**
- Pushing the device away
- Covering eyes or ears
- Saying "no," "stop," or "all done"
- Leaving the area
- Shutting down or going quiet

Treat **no** and **stop** as valid AAC messages—even when inconvenient.

## Practical assent habits

1. **Ask before physical help.** "Can I help?" Model **yes** / **no** symbols.
2. **Offer choices, not commands.** "Water or juice?" with two symbols—not one forced option.
3. **Use wait time.** After modeling, count silently to five before prompting again.
4. **Celebrate refusal.** "You said **stop**. I will pause." This builds trust.
5. **Caregiver co-regulation first.** If you are rushed or frustrated, pause AAC practice.

## Toddler-specific

- Keep sessions **under 2 minutes** at first.
- Use **large tiles**, soft colors, no flashing animation.
- A caregiver reads aloud; the toddler does not need to perform.

## Teen and adult-specific

- Do not require eye contact to accept a message.
- **Privacy please** and **I don't consent** must be honored immediately.
- Autonomy includes choosing *not* to use AAC in a given moment.
